All On Board

It is essential to remain up-to-date on user feedback and a user’s experience. By collaborating with a team and working with Warp, we were able to take their previously received client feedback to design and improve the onboarding experience of their modernized terminal.
Role: Designer
Team: 4 Designers, guidance from 2 Lead Designers
Timeline: April 2024
WARP'S REIMAGINED TERMINAL
Warp’s mission is to re-create the terminal and command line experience in a modern, efficient, and more usable app. They have incorporated features such as the Warp drive for collaboration, Warp AI, and AI auto-correction to reimagine a user’s terminal.
OUR TASK
Our team of four was explicitly tasked with altering the onboarding experience. The current onboarding process starts with a marketing survey and is followed by a brief tutorial on some features in their organizational structure of “blocklists.” We focused on improving and expanding the sequence to keep users engaged and well-informed on all of the unique features. Through our work, Warp sought to streamline user setup and minimize the time needed to determine if the product was right for them.
ONBOARDING RESEARCH
To begin our remodeling of the onboarding experience, we read through Warp’s product brief and feature list to further understand what features they wanted us to handle and the users they were looking to target. I went through the downloading and onboarding process and took notes about what features made sense and what I found particularly confusing. We reached out to the design team and asked for clarifications on company jargon and the motivation of the product. We broadened our research to consider how other companies handled their onboarding experience and what enjoyable and reliable experiences we could implement in our revised onboarding prototype.
BEGINNING OF DESIGN
Our team set out to create eight independent sketches, two per person. One design was relatively straightforward, while the other was more far-fetched and elaborate. We spent a lot of time analyzing and determining what features made the most sense from each of our sketches to include in the final development. Specifically, from my research and sketching, we decided to incorporate hidden but distinctive features that were missing in the onboarding experience, such as the Warp drive and the AI features.
SKETCHES
ORIGINAL LOW-FI DESIGN
After discussing which features to incorporate, we designed our first low-fi wireframe. We adjusted the login process to differentiate the text of logging in and signing up. We also moved the sign-up screen and marketing survey into the Warp terminal app itself but kept these features separate models. Our goal was to keep these two components together but be distinct from the command-line interaction within the terminal. We adjusted the marketing survey to remove the user's need to click the "Next" button in the survey and instead replaced it with an automatic advance of the questions. We decided to keep the "in-blocklist" experience once the user reaches the terminal, meaning the appearance of a typical scrolling command-by-command terminal UI with the addition of selectable blocks. We expanded on Warp's existing configuration steps to allow users to select their shell to reduce confusion about porting over their existing setup. We then walked through Warp's features: we attempted to balance existing onboarding components and introduce different features to promote the app's uniqueness.
LOW-FI MOCKUP
FIRST ROUND CRITIQUES
We sent our initial wireframe to the designers to receive feedback on the direction we were heading. We also shared with our fellow peers to receive advice on our general formatting and sketches. The main critiques from non-warp users were rooted in design: removing unnecessary whitespace and keeping each frame in the wireframe consistent with the actual user process of the “in-blocklist” design. We also received advice to mark a user’s progress in the marketing survey or the entire onboarding experience to keep users engaged and up-to-date on the timing of the process. Lastly, our client expressed their interest in having the marketing survey moved into the “blocklist” structure in the terminal.
REWORKING
We moved to adapt and create a second low-fi wireframe on which we could base our high-fi prototype and start to develop a style guide. We split up the frames to develop, organize, and flesh out the login/sign-up flow into the marketing survey in the “blocklist” design, moving to allow users to choose their configurations. From here, the experience delves into the unique features, talking about creating commands, demonstrating a user’s drive, and the inventive ideas of AI incorporation. I specifically focused on including the features that were not previously highlighted, such as the Drive and AI. I looked at how these features were previously presented to users and moved to adapt these ideas into the onboarding process. We took these ideas and elaborated them into a high-fi prototype.
